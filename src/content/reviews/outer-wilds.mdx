Outer Wilds is difficult to describe without diminishing it. On the surface, it is a space exploration game with a 22-minute time loop. In practice, it is one of the most carefully designed knowledge puzzles ever built.

## What it is

You play as an astronaut launching into a small, handcrafted solar system. Every planet has its own physics, its own hazards, and its own part of a larger mystery. After 22 minutes, the sun explodes and the loop resets. You keep nothing except what you have learned.

There are no upgrades, no unlocks, no skill trees. The only thing that changes between loops is your understanding of the world. The game is complete the moment you know enough to reach the ending.

## What works

The solar system is remarkably dense. Each planet is small enough to explore on foot, but layered with interconnected secrets. Clues found on one planet explain phenomena on another. The ship log tracks what you have discovered and how it connects, turning scattered observations into a coherent picture.

The time loop is not a gimmick. It is a design constraint that makes exploration feel urgent without being punishing. You learn to plan routes, prioritize questions, and accept that some loops will end before you finish what you started. That rhythm becomes part of the experience.

The writing is restrained and effective. There are no cutscenes, no voice acting, and almost no exposition. The story is told through environment, architecture, and short text logs left behind by an ancient civilization. It trusts the player to piece things together.

## What could be better

The controls can be frustrating, especially in zero gravity and tight spaces. Flying the ship has a steep learning curve that occasionally turns exploration into a fight with the physics engine.

A few key discoveries depend on being in the right place at the right time within the loop. If you do not know when to look, you can spend several loops waiting or retrying without making progress.

## Who it is for

Outer Wilds is for anyone who finds satisfaction in understanding a system by observing it closely. It rewards curiosity more directly than almost any game I have played. If you enjoy figuring things out without being told what to look for, this is essential.
